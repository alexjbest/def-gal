Section 1 Background

¶

Let \(E_k\) denote the Eisenstein series of weight \(k\text{,}\) \(k\gt 2\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} E_k = \frac{-B_{k}}{2k} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \sigma _{k-1} (n) q^n \in M_k(\SL_2(\ZZ))\text{.} \end{equation*}

Where \(B_k\)] are the Bernoulli numbers and

\begin{equation*} \sigma _{k-1} (n) = \sum_{d|n,\, d\gt 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}

\(E_2\) however is not holomorphic, so not a modular form.

Fix \(N\) a prime, notation has stuck from Mazur's Eisenstein ideal paper.

Then there exists a unique Eisenstein series on \(\Gamma _0(N)\) of weight 2.

\begin{equation*} E_2^{(N)} = \frac{N-1}{12} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \sigma (n) q^n\text{.} \end{equation*}

Funny observation: if \(N \equiv 1 \pmod p\) for prime \(p \gt 3\text{.}\) Then \(p | ((N-1)/12)\text{,}\) so \(E_2^{(N)}\) “looks cuspidal”.

Then we hope that there exists a cuspidal eigenform \(f \in S_2(\Gamma _0(N))\) such that

\begin{equation*} f \equiv E_2^{(N)} ``\pmod p ''\text{.} \end{equation*}

This is in fact true, due to Koike in the 70's, there exists \(f \in S_2(\Gamma _0(N))\) such that

\begin{equation*} a_\ell (f) \equiv 1+ \ell \pmod p \end{equation*}

for all \(\ell \ne N,p\text{.}\)

Question 1.1.

How many such \(f\) are there?

Merel '96:

\begin{equation*} f \text{ is unique} \iff \prod_{i=1}^{(N-1)/2} i^i \text{ is not a }p\text{-th power modulo }N\text{.} \end{equation*}

Wake and Wang-Erickson describe the dimension of the space of such \(f\) using Massey products (higher cup products).

Method: Galois deformations!

Subsection 1.1 Galois representations

We write

\begin{equation*} G_{\QQ} = \absgal{\QQ} = \varprojlim_{F/\QQ,\text{fin. galois}} \Gal F\QQ \end{equation*}

a profinite group.

\begin{equation*} \rho \colon G_{\QQ} \to \GL_2 (\QQ_p) \end{equation*}

a continuous homomorphism. Then view \(\GL_2(\QQ_p)\) as \(\Aut(V)\) for a 2-dimensional \(\QQ_p\) vector space and fix a 2-dimensional \(\ZZ_p\)-lattice

\begin{equation*} T \subseteq V \end{equation*}

which is \(G_{\QQ}\) stable. Then we can take

\begin{equation*} \bar \rho \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(\FF_p) \end{equation*}

this is unique (w.r.t. the choice of \(T\)) only up to semisimplification.

So we say two Galois representations \(\rho _1,\rho _2\) are congruent if

\begin{equation*} \bar \rho _1^\semis \simeq \bar \rho _2^\semis\text{.} \end{equation*}

We say \(\rho _1,\rho _2\) are deformations of

\begin{equation*} \bar \rho _1= \bar \rho _2 \end{equation*}

(imagine this is reducible).

Start with

\begin{equation*} \bar \rho \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(\FF_p) \end{equation*}

consider “all” deformations of \(\bar\rho \) in good cases there exists a “universal” deformation of \(\bar \rho \text{.}\)

\(R^{\mathrm{univ}}\) a local ring with maximal ideal \(\ideal m_R\) such that

\begin{equation*} R/\ideal m_R = \FF_p\text{.} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \rho ^{\mathrm{univ}} \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(R^{\mathrm{univ}}) \end{equation*}

such that if \(\rho \colon G_\QQ \to GL_2(R)\) is a deformation of \(\bar \rho \) then there exists

\begin{equation*} R^{\mathrm{univ}} \to R \end{equation*}

such that

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ G_\QQ \ar[r]^{\rho ^{\mathrm{univ}}} \ar[dr]_\rho & \GL_2(R^{\mathrm{univ}})\ar[d] \\ & GL_2(R) }\text{.} \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.2 Modular forms

\begin{equation*} f = \sum a_n q^n \in S_k(\Gamma _0(N)) \end{equation*}

an eigenform leads to

\begin{equation*} \rho _f \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(K),\,K/\QQ_p \text{ finite} \end{equation*}

with the property that for all \(\ell \nmid Np\) we have

\begin{equation*} \trace(\rho _f(Frob_\ell)) = a_\ell\text{.} \end{equation*}

Modular forms can be congruent

\begin{equation*} a_\ell (f_1) \equiv a_\ell(f_2) \pmod p \text{ for all but finitely many } \ell \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \Updownarrow \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \bar \rho _{f_1}^\semis\simeq \bar \rho _{f_2}^\semis\text{.} \end{equation*}

There exists a ring, the Hecke algebra \(\mathbf T\) parametrizing all \(f\)'s with the same \(\bar \rho \text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} f \leadsto \rho _f \implies R^{\mathrm{univ}} \to \mathbf T \end{equation*}

so hope

\begin{equation*} R^{\mathrm{univ}} \simeq \mathbf T\text{.} \end{equation*}

Wiles proof of FLT proved one of these.

Many more such theorems in the past couple of decades.

Wake and Wang-Erickson show that the dimension of

\begin{equation*} \{ f : f \equiv E_2^{(N)}\} \leftrightarrow \rank \mathbf T = \rank R^{\mathrm{univ}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} a_\ell(f) \equiv 1+ \ell \pmod p \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \implies \bar \rho ^\semis = \mathbf 1 \oplus \mathbf \mu _p \end{equation*}

but there does not exist \(R^{\mathrm{univ}}\) in this context.

The fix is to use pseudorepresentations instead of representations.

Subsection 1.3 Pseudorepresentations

Let \(G\) be a group.

Then a pseudorepresentation \(T\) is a map

\begin{equation*} T\colon G \to A \end{equation*}

for \(A\) a ring satisfying

  1. \begin{equation*} T(xy) = T(yx) \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} T(x)T(y)T(z) - T(x)T(yz) - T(y)T(xz) - T(z)T(xy) + T(xyz) + T(xzy) = 0 \end{equation*}

and the analogous formulae for higher products.

Fact 1.2.

If \(A\) is an algebraically closed field of characteristic \(\ne 2\text{.}\) Then for a given pseudorepresentation \(T\) there exists a true representation \(\rho \) such that

\begin{equation*} T = \trace(\rho )\text{.} \end{equation*}

But this does not hold in general.

Universal pseudodeformation rings always exist. Wake and Wang-Erickson use \(R^{\mathrm{univ}} =\) universal pseudodeformation ring.
