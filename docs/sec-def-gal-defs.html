Subsection 2.1 Representations

Lecture 2 23/1/2018

Definition 2.1. Representations.

Let \(G\) be a finite group and \(V\) a finite dimensional vector space over \(\CC\) of dimension \(d\text{.}\) A representation of \(G\) is a homomorphism

\begin{equation*} G \xrightarrow\rho \Aut(V) \simeq \GL_d(\CC)\text{,} \end{equation*}

\(G\) acts linearly on \(V\text{.}\)

Galois representations: Let \(G\) be a Galois group, possibly infinite. \(F\) be a field,

\begin{equation*} G_F = \absgal F = \varprojlim_{L/F,\text{ fin. gal.}} \Gal L F \end{equation*}

profinite compact and totally disconnected.

Replace \(V\) with a finite free module over some topological ring \(A\)

\begin{equation*} \rho \colon G_F \to \GL_d(A) \end{equation*}

a continuous homomorphism.

Example 2.2.

\(A = \CC\) with the complex topology.

Fact 2.3.

In this case \(\im(\rho )\) is finite.

Exercise 2.4.

Prove this.

Then we can write

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ G_F \ar[rr]^{\rho} \ar[dr] & & \GL_d(\CC) \\ & \Gal L F \ar[ur] & } \end{equation*}

where \(L/F\) is finite. There are many such representations.

Conjecture 2.5.

Every finite group is a quotient of \(G_\QQ\text{.}\)

Example 2.6.
\begin{equation*} F= \QQ \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} L = \QQ(\sqrt[4]{2}, i) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \Gal L \QQ \simeq D_4 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} G_\QQ \to \Gal L \QQ \xrightarrow\rho \GL_2(\CC) \end{equation*}

with \(\rho \) the unique irreducible 2-dimensional representation of \(D_4\text{.}\)

Example 2.7.

Let \(E/\QQ\) be an elliptic curve

\begin{equation*} G_\QQ \acts E\lb p\rb \simeq \ZZ/p \oplus \ZZ/p \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \rho _{E,p} \colon G_\QQ \to \Aut(E\lb p\rb ) \simeq \GL_2(\FF_p) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \rho _{E,p^n} \colon G_\QQ \to \Aut(E\lb p^n\rb ) \simeq \GL_2(\ZZ/p^n) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \rho _{E,p^\infty} \colon G_\QQ \to \Aut(E\lb p^\infty\rb ) \simeq \GL_2(\ZZ_p)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Fact 2.8.

\(\rho _{E,p^\infty}\) has finite image.

Exercise 2.9.

Prove this.

Example 2.10.

Let \(F = \QQ_\ell\text{,}\) \(A= \QQ_p\)

\begin{equation*} \rho \colon G_{\QQ_\ell} \to \GL_d(\QQ_p)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The Galois theory of local fields looks like

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ \overline{\QQ}_\ell \ar@{-}[d]^{I_{\ell}^w} \ar@{-}@/_3pc/[ddd]_{\mathrm{G}_\ell} \ar@{-}@/_1.75pc/[dd]^{I_{\ell}} & \\ \QQ_\ell^\mathrm{tr} \ar@{-}[d]^{\prod_{q\ne \ell} \ZZ_q(1)} & \QQ_\ell^\ab \\ \QQ_\ell^\ur \ar@{-}[d]^{\widehat \ZZ} \ar@{-}@/_1.5pc/[ur]_{\ZZ_\ell^*} & \\ \QQ_\ell & } \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} \QQ_\ell^\mathrm{tr}= \QQ_\ell(\{\sqrt[n]{\ell}\}_{\ell\nmid n}) \end{equation*}

the maximal tamely ramified extension

\begin{equation*} \QQ_\ell^\ur = \QQ_\ell(\{\mu _{n}\}_{\ell\nmid n}) \end{equation*}

the maximal unramified extension

\begin{equation*} \QQ_\ell^\ab = \QQ_\ell^\un(\mu _{\ell^\infty }) \end{equation*}

the maximal abelian extension.

\begin{equation*} I_\ell = \Gal { \overline \QQ_\ell}{\QQ_\ell^\ur} \end{equation*}

the intertia group

\begin{equation*} I^w_\ell = \Gal { \overline \QQ_\ell}{\QQ_\ell^\tamer} \end{equation*}

the wild intertia group

We say

\begin{equation*} \rho \colon G_{\QQ_\ell} \to \GL_d(\QQ_p) \end{equation*}

is unramified if

\begin{equation*} \rho (I_\ell) = \{1\} \end{equation*}

is tamely ramified if

\begin{equation*} \rho (I_\ell^w) = \{1\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the first case \(\rho \) is completely determined by \(\rho (\Frob_\ell)\text{.}\) In the second case \(\rho \) is completely determined by \(\rho (\Frob_\ell)\) and its value on a generator of

\begin{equation*} \Gal { \QQ_\ell^\tamer}{\QQ_\ell^\ur}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The wild part: \(I_\ell^w\) is pro-\(\ell\text{,}\) \(\GL_d(\ZZ_p)\) is almost pro-\(p\) (it has a finite index pro-\(p\) subgroup).

Exercise 2.11.

Prove this.

Example 2.12.

For \(d= 1\)

\begin{equation*} \ZZ_p^* = \FF_p^* \times (1 + p\ZZ_p)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus if \(\ell \ne p\) then

\begin{equation*} \rho (I_\ell^w)\subseteq \GL_d(\QQ_p) \end{equation*}

is finite.

Exercise 2.13.

Prove this.

If \(\ell = p\) then this is handled by \(p\)-adic Hodge theory.

The connection to global representations is then that

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ \overline \QQ \ar[r] & \overline \QQ_\ell \\ \QQ \ar[r]\ar[u] & \QQ_\ell\ar[u]} \end{equation*}

So

\begin{equation*} G_{\QQ_\ell} \hookrightarrow G_\QQ \end{equation*}

via restriction to \(\overline \QQ\text{.}\)

The image of this map is the decomposition group at \(\ell\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \rho \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_d(A)\text{,} \end{equation*}

we say that \(\rho \) is unramified at \(\ell\) if

\begin{equation*} \rho (I_\ell) = \{1\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In which case

\begin{equation*} \operatorname{charpoly}(\rho (\Frob_\ell)) \end{equation*}

is well-defined.

Returning to

\begin{equation*} \rho _{E,p^\infty} \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(\QQ_p) \end{equation*}

now.

Fact 2.14.

\(\rho _{E,p^\infty}\) is unramified outside of \(N_E\cdot p\) (\(N_E\) is the conductor of \(E\)). i.e.

\begin{equation*} \rho _{E,p^\infty} \end{equation*}

is unramified at \(\ell\) if and only if \(\ell \ne p\) and \(\ell\) is a prime of good reduction for \(E\text{.}\)

So \(\rho _{E,p^\infty} \) sees bad reduction.

From \(\rho _{E,p^\infty} \) you can recover \(E\) up to isogeny (Faltings).

Example 2.15.
\begin{equation*} G_\QQ\acts \mu _{p^n} \end{equation*}

so we get

\begin{equation*} G_\QQ \to \Aut(\mu _{p^n}) \simeq (\ZZ/p^n\ZZ)^* \simeq \GL_1(\ZZ/p^n) \end{equation*}

taking the inverse limit we get

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ G_\QQ \ar[rr]^{\epsilon _p} \ar[dr] & & \ZZ_p^*\\ & \Gal {\QQ(\mu _{p^\infty })} \QQ \ar[ur]_{\sim} & } \end{equation*}

this \(\epsilon _p\) is known as the \(p\)-adic cyclotomic character. This is unramified outside \(p\) and

\begin{equation*} \epsilon _p (\Frob_\ell) = \ell \end{equation*}

for \(\ell \ne p\text{.}\)

Remark 2.16.
\begin{equation*} \det(\rho _{E,p^\infty}) = \epsilon _p\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 2.17.
\begin{equation*} f = \sum a_nq^n \in S_2(\Gamma _0(N), \QQ) \end{equation*}

a weight 2 eigenform on \(\Gamma _0(N)\text{,}\) with rational fourier coefficients. Eichler-Shimura gives \(E_f/\QQ\) an elliptic curve. Define

\begin{equation*} \rho _f = \rho _{E,p^\infty }\colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(\QQ_p) \end{equation*}

unramified outside \(Np\)

\begin{equation*} \trace(\rho (\Frob_\ell)) = a_\ell \end{equation*}

for \(\ell\nmid Np\text{.}\) More generally

\begin{equation*} f \in S_2(\Gamma _0(N)) \end{equation*}

an eigenform, Eichler-Shimura gives

\begin{equation*} A_f/\QQ \end{equation*}

an abelian variety which leads to

\begin{equation*} \rho _f \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(K) \end{equation*}

\(K/\QQ_p\) finite.

Subsection 2.2 Congruences and elliptic curves

Let

\begin{equation*} E_1\colon y^2 = x^3 + x - 10 \end{equation*}

conductor \(2^2 \cdot13\text{,}\) https://www.lmfdb.org/EllipticCurve/Q/52a1/

\begin{equation*} E_2\colon y^2 = x^3 -584 x + 5444 \end{equation*}

conductor \(2^2 \cdot7\cdot13\text{,}\) https://www.lmfdb.org/EllipticCurve/Q/364a1/.

Note that

\begin{equation*} a_\ell(E_1) \equiv a_\ell(E_2) \pmod 5,\,\forall \ell \ne 7 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \implies \rho _{E_1, 5} \simeq\rho _{E_2, 5} ( = \bar \rho ) \end{equation*}

as Galois representations.

Exercise 2.19.

Prove this.

How common is this? We have 2 lifts of \(\bar \rho \)

\begin{equation*} \rho _{E_1, 5^\infty } \simeq\rho _{E_2, 5^\infty } \end{equation*}

how many other such?

Subsection 2.3 Hida theory

\begin{equation*} \sum a_n(f) q^n = f \in S_{k_0} (\Gamma _0(N)) \end{equation*}

an eigenform.

\begin{equation*} a_p(f) \end{equation*}

a \(p\)-adic unit.

\begin{equation*} \mathscr F = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n(k) q^n \end{equation*}

with \(a_n\) a \(p\)-adic analytic function in \(k\text{.}\) The whole family gives Galois representations that reduce to the same \(\bar\rho \text{.}\)

Specialise \(k\) to some integer \(w\)

\begin{equation*} \mathscr F = \sum a_n(w) q^n \in S_w (\Gamma _0(N)) \end{equation*}

take \(w= k_0\) to recover \(f\text{.}\)

Hida constructs

\begin{equation*} \rho ^{Hida}\colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(\ZZ_p\lb \lb x\rb \rb ) \end{equation*}

unramified outside \(Np\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \ell \nmid Np \implies \tr(\rho^{Hida} (\Frob_\ell)) = a_\ell(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Subsection 2.4 More examples of Galois representations in families

A 1-dimensional family.

let

\begin{equation*} \epsilon _p = \text{cyclotomic character} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \epsilon _p\colon G_\QQ \to \Gal{\QQ(\mu _{p^\infty })}{\QQ} \simeq \ZZ_p^* \simeq \FF_p^* \times (1+p\ZZ_p) \end{equation*}

If we have \(k \in \ZZ\) we can take

\begin{equation*} \epsilon _p^k \end{equation*}

its power.

Note that when \(k_1,k_2\in \ZZ\) with

\begin{equation*} k_1\equiv k_2 \pmod{p-1} \end{equation*}

then

\begin{equation*} \epsilon _p^{k_1} \equiv \epsilon _p^{k_2} \pmod{p} \end{equation*}

moreover if

\begin{equation*} k_1\equiv k_2 \pmod{(p-1)p^N} \end{equation*}

we havee

\begin{equation*} \epsilon _p^{k_1} \equiv \epsilon _p^{k_2} \pmod{p^{N+1}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Set

\begin{equation*} \Lambda = \ZZ_p\lb \lb \ZZ_p^*\rb \rb = \varprojlim \ZZ_p\lb (\ZZ/p^N)^*\rb \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \epsilon _p^{univ}\colon G_\QQ \to \Lambda ^* = \GL_1(\Lambda ) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \sigma \mapsto \lb epsilon _p(\sigma ) \rb \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \Lambda \xrightarrow{wt_k} \ZZ_p \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} wt_k \circ \epsilon _p^{univ} \end{equation*}

where \(wt_k\) is defined by

\begin{equation*} \ZZ_p^* \to \ZZ_p^* \subseteq \ZZ_p \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} x \mapsto x^k \end{equation*}

gives

\begin{equation*} \Lambda \xrightarrow{wt_k} \ZZ_p\text{.} \end{equation*}

Take any

\begin{equation*} \phi \in \Hom_{cts}(\Lambda , \CC_p)\simeq \Hom_{cts}(\ZZ_p^*, \CC_p) \end{equation*}

induces

\begin{equation*} G_\QQ \to \CC_p^* \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \phi \circ \epsilon _p^{univ}\text{.} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \Hom_{cts}(\ZZ_p^*, \CC_p) \end{equation*}

is a union of \(p-1\) open disks as

\begin{equation*} \ZZ_p^* \simeq \FF_p^* \times (1+p\ZZ_p) \end{equation*}

the rightmost factor is topologically generated by \(1+p\text{.}\) So

\begin{equation*} \phi \colon \ZZ_p^* \to \CC_p^* \end{equation*}

is determined by

\begin{equation*} \phi |_{\FF_p^*} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \phi (\gamma ) \text{ for any } \gamma \text{ a top. gen.}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We need

\begin{equation*} |\phi (\gamma )-1|_p \lt 1\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have \(p-1\) disks labelled by characters of \(\FF_p^*\text{.}\) On each disk of \(\phi \in \Hom(\ZZ_p^*, \CC_p^*)\) the reduction of

\begin{equation*} \phi \circ \epsilon _p^{univ} \colon G_\QQ \to \CC_p^* \end{equation*}

is the same.

A 2-d example.

Let \(E/\QQ\) be a CM elliptic curve.

\begin{equation*} \rho_{E,p^\infty } \colon G_\QQ \to \GL_2(\ZZ_p) \end{equation*}

then there exists \(K/\QQ\) quadratic imaginary field and , \(p\)-ordinary

\begin{equation*} \psi \colon G_K \to \ZZ_p^* \end{equation*}

s.t.

\begin{equation*} \rho_{E,p^\infty } \simeq \Ind_K^\QQ(\psi )\text{.} \end{equation*}

So we get a “family”

\begin{equation*} \Ind_K^\QQ(\psi ^k)\text{,} \end{equation*}

more generally can make

\begin{equation*} \psi ^{univ} \colon G_K \to \Lambda ^* \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \Ind \psi ^{univ} \colon G_K \to \GL_2(\Lambda )\text{.} \end{equation*}

CM Hida family.

Subsection 2.5 Deformation theory

\(G\) a profinite group, \(k\) a finite field of characteristic \(p\) (simpler \(k = \FF_p\)).

\begin{equation*} \bar \rho \colon G \to \GL_d(k) \end{equation*}

a continuous homomorphism.

To lift \(\bar \rho \) to be \(A\)-valued, we need a homomorphism

\begin{equation*} A \twoheadrightarrow k \end{equation*}

we may as well assume \(A \) is local so it lifts to \(GL_d(A)\text{.}\) We then only have one residual representation. Localize it at

\begin{equation*} \ker(A\to k)\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(A\) is a local ring then \(A\) has a natural topology. Letting \(\ideal m _A\) be the maximal ideal

\begin{equation*} \{\ideal m_A^j\} \end{equation*}

is a neighborhood base at 0, this is the \(\ideal m\)-adic topology.

So we have \(A\) local, \(\ideal m_A\) maximal \(A/\ideal m_A \simeq k\text{,}\) we need to fix this identification to ensure we don't have automorphisms.

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ G \ar[r]^{\rho } \ar[dr]_{\bar\rho }& \GL_d(A)\ar[d]\\ & \GL_d(k) }\text{.} \end{equation*}
Note 2.20.

If we have \(M\in \GL_d(A)\) s.t. \(M\equiv 1 \pmod {\ideal m_A}\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} M\rho M\inv \end{equation*}

lifts \(\bar\rho \) also.

Definition 2.21.

Call \(\rho \) and \(M \rho M\inv \) strictly equivalent.

Definition 2.22.

A deformation of \(\bar\rho\) to A is a strict equivalence class of continuous homomorphisms

\begin{equation*} G \xrightarrow{\rho } \GL_d(A) \end{equation*}

lifting \(\bar \rho \text{.}\)

Want to rephrase this with less bases, instead of \(\bar \rho \) use \(\overline V\) a \(d\)-dimensional continuous representation of \(G\) over \(k\text{.}\) \(V\) a free \(A\)-module of rank \(d\) with continuous action of \(G\) s.t.

\begin{equation*} V\otimes A/\ideal m _A \simeq \overline V\text{.} \end{equation*}

Naive hope: two such \(V\)'s are equivalent if they are isomorphic as \(G\)-modules.

Problem: \(R^{univ}\) won't exist, too many automorphisms. Ideally if \(V_A\) is a deformation of \(\overline V\) to \(A\) then there exists a unique

\begin{equation*} R^{univ} \to A \end{equation*}

inducing

\begin{equation*} V^{univ } \to V_A\text{.} \end{equation*}

Take II, let \(V\) be a free \(A\)-module with a continuous action of \(G\) and a fixed isomorphism.

\begin{equation*} V\otimes _A A/\ideal m _A \simeq \overline V \end{equation*}

as representations of \(G\) over \(k\simeq A/\ideal m_A\text{.}\)

Two \(V\)'s are equivalent if there exists a \(G\)-module isomorphism \(V_1\xrightarrow\phi V_2\) s.t.

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ V_1\ar[r]^\phi\ar[d] & V_2\ar[d] \\ V_1\otimes A/\ideal m_A\ar[d]_\sim & V_2\otimes A/\ideal m_A \ar[d]_sim \\ \overline V\ar[r]^= &\overline V }\text{.} \end{equation*}
Exercise 2.23.

Check the two definitions are the same, \(\GL_d\) definition vs. \(\overline V\text{.}\)

Another approach

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ G \ar[r]^{\rho } \ar[dr]_{\bar\rho }& \GL_d(A)\ar[d]\\ & \GL_d(k) } \end{equation*}

lift of \(\bar \rho \) and say two \(\rho \)'s are the same if they are equal as maps, this is a framed deformation. We can write this more abstractly

\begin{equation*} \overline V \end{equation*}

a continuous representation of \(G\) over \(k\) with fixed basis \(\beta \text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} V \end{equation*}

a free \(A\) module with cont. action of \(G\) and a fixed isom.

\begin{equation*} V\otimes_A A/\ideal m_D \simeq \overline V \end{equation*}

and a basis \(\beta _A\) lifting \(\beta \text{.}\)

Categories

\begin{equation*} \mathcal C_k =\mathcal C \end{equation*}

the category of complete local Noetherian rings \(A\) with a fixed isomorphism \(A/\ideal m_A \simeq k\text{.}\) Where the maps are

\begin{equation*} A_1\xrightarrow\phi A_2 \end{equation*}

a local ring homomorphism

\begin{equation*} \phi (\ideal m_{A_1}) \subseteq \ideal m_{A_2} \end{equation*}

and the diagram

\begin{equation*} \xymatrix{ A_1/\ideal m_{A_1} \ar[rr]^{\bar \phi } \ar[dr]_\sim & & A_2/\ideal m_{A_2}\ar[dl]^\sim \\ & k & } \end{equation*}

commutes.
